Albert Einstein

Early Life

Albert Einstein was born on March 14, 1879, in Ulm, in the Kingdom of Württemberg (Germany). His parents were part of a secular, middle-class Jewish family.

Family Background
    Father – Hermann Einstein
  • Engineer and entrepreneur with a small electrical company.
    • Mother – Pauline Koch
  • Highly cultured, musical, strict with education.
They later moved to Munich, where Einstein spent his childhood.
Einstein Spoke slowly as a child (leading to the exaggerated myth of learning disabilities), Was introverted, thoughtful, and curious,Hated rigid schooling but loved math, science, and music,Learned violin from his mother, which became a lifelong love, Built mechanical devices and conducted simple experiments

Education

At age 15, Einstein Clashed with Germany’s authoritarian school system,Left Munich to join his family in Italy Renounced his German citizenship at 16 (to avoid military service),

Swiss Education
He applied to the Swiss Federal Polytechnic (ETH) in Zurich in 1895. Though he failed all subjects except math and physics, the school let him enroll after completing high school in Aarau, Switzerland.
At ETH Zurich (1896–1900)
Einstein studied:
  • Physics
  • Mathematics
  • Electromagnetism
  • Advanced calculus
  • Mechanics
He graduated in 1900 but wasn’t seen as an obedient or promising student. Professors disliked his independent attitude, so he couldn’t get a job in academia.In 1902, with help from a friend, he secured a job as a technical assistant (patent examiner) at the Swiss Patent Office in Bern. While working full-time, Einstein completed a PhD and published four revolutionary papers in the journal Annalen der Physik, reshaping modern physics:
  • Photoelectric Effect – introduced the idea of light quanta (photons), laying foundations for quantum theory.
  • Brownian Motion – provided empirical proof for atoms.
  • Special Theory of Relativity – showed time and space are relative and introduced the concept of spacetime.
  • Mass–Energy Equivalence – from relativity came the famous equation E = mc².
These works are collectively known as the Annus Mirabilis Pa

Personal Life

Married Mileva Marić in 1903; they had two sons (Hans Albert and Eduard) and an earlier daughter, Lieserl, whose fate remains unclear.Divorced in 1919. Married his cousin Elsa Einstein in 1919. Known for his eccentric personality, love for sailing, music, and strong yet independent political views.

Death

Einstein died on 18 April 1955 at Princeton Hospital from an abdominal aortic aneurysm. He refused surgery, saying: I want to go when I want. It is tasteless to prolong life artificially. He was cremated, and his ashes were scattered at a secret location. His brain was preserved (controversially) by the autopsy doctor for scientific study.

Nelson Mandela

Early Life

Nelson Rolihlahla Mandela was born on 18 July 1918 in Mvezo, a small village in the Eastern Cape of South Africa. He belonged to the Madiba clan of the Xhosa-speaking Thembu people, a royal lineage. His father, Gadla Henry Mphakanyiswa, was a chief and councilor to the Thembu king. His mother, Nonqaphi Nosekeni, was one of the chief's four wives. Mandela’s birth name, Rolihlahla, roughly translates to “troublemaker”, a name that would later take on symbolic meaning in his political life. After his father’s death when he was nine years old, Mandela was placed under the guardianship of the Thembu regent, Chief Jongintaba Dalindyebo, who raised him in the royal household at Mqhekezweni. Growing up, Mandela listened to the elders’ stories of his ancestors’ brave resistance to colonial rule, planting in him a lifelong yearning for justice and freedom.

Education and Political Awakening

Mandela was the first in his family to attend school, where a teacher gave him the English name Nelson. He completed his secondary education at Healdtown, a Methodist school known for strict discipline and quality teaching. He then enrolled at the University of Fort Hare, one of the few higher-education institutions available to Black South Africans at the time. At Fort Hare, his political awareness began to grow. He was suspended for participating in a student protest against university policies. To avoid a forced marriage arranged by his guardian, Mandela fled to Johannesburg in 1941, working as a night watchman and later as a law clerk. He completed a Bachelor of Arts degree by correspondence through the University of South Africa (UNISA). In 1943, he began studying law at the University of Witwatersrand, where he was one of the few Black students and encountered future anti-apartheid activists.
In 1944, Mandela joined the African National Congress (ANC), helping to found its Youth League (ANCYL) to encourage a more radical and confrontational approach to oppressive government policies.
Key developments:

  • Formation of the ANC Youth League
  • Mandela, along with Walter Sisulu and Oliver Tambo, promoted mass action, civil disobedience, and boycotts.
  • They challenged the older, moderate leadership of the ANC.
    • Defiance Campaign (1952)
    Mandela became a leading figure in the 1952 Defiance Campaign, which encouraged peaceful resistance against apartheid laws. He was arrested and received a suspended sentence under the Suppression of Communism Act. Establishment of South Africa’s first Black law firm That same year, Mandela and Oliver Tambo opened Mandela & Tambo, providing affordable legal defense to Black South Africans oppressed under apartheid.
    The Freedom Charter (1955)
    Mandela helped organize the Congress of the People, which adopted the Freedom Charter, laying the ideological foundation for the anti-apartheid movement.

Armed Struggle and Imprisionment

Turning to Armed Resistance

Following the Sharpeville Massacre (1960), Mandela concluded that peaceful protests alone would not defeat apartheid. He co-founded Umkhonto we Sizwe (MK), the ANC’s armed wing, advocating sabotage over violence against people.

Arrest and the Rivonia Trial (1964)

In 1962, Mandela was arrested and sentenced to 5 years for leaving the country illegally. During police raids, documents linking him to MK activities were discovered, leading to the Rivonia Trial.
He was sentenced to life imprisonment, spending:
  • 18 years on Robben Island
  • 6 years at Pollsmoor Prison
  • the remainder at Victor Verster Prison
Mandela became a worldwide symbol of resistance. International campaigns demanded his release.
Mandela was released on 11 February 1990, after 27 years in prison, following global pressure and internal political change. He immediately resumed political leadership and Became President of the ANC, Led negotiations with President F.W. de Klerk, Helped dismantle apartheid laws, Oversaw creation of the first democratic elections, In 1993, Mandela and de Klerk jointly received the Nobel Peace Prize.

Presidency

In April 1994, South Africa held its first multiracial democratic election. Mandela became the first Black President of South Africa, serving from 1994 to 1999.
Key achievements:

    Truth and Reconciliation Commission (TRC)

  • Chaired by Archbishop Desmond Tutu, it investigated apartheid-era crimes and emphasized restorative justice.
  • Nation-Building and Reconciliation.
  • Mandela pushed for racial unity and forgiveness rather than revenge.
  • Social and Economic Reforms

    • Efforts to expand healthcare and education

  • Housing programs for the poor.
  • New constitution guaranteeing equal rights

    • International Diplomacy

  • Mandela was a global ambassador for peace and humanitarian causes.

Death

Nelson Mandela died on 5 December 2013 in Johannesburg at the age of 95 from a lung infection. He received a state funeral attended by presidents, prime ministers, royalty, and millions watching worldwide. His legacy remains one of the strongest symbols of freedom and justice in modern history.

Winston Churchill

Early Life

Winston Leonard Spencer Churchill was born on 30 November 1874 at Blenheim Palace in Oxfordshire, England.

    He came from an aristocratic family:
  • His father, Lord Randolph Churchill, was a prominent Conservative politician.
  • His mother, Jennie Jerome, was an American socialite from a wealthy New York family.
Churchill grew up in a household where political discussion and elite society were constant influences. Despite this, his early academic career was unimpressive. He struggled in traditional school settings but excelled in subjects that captured his imagination, such as English, history, and writing.

Harrow School

At age 12, he was admitted to Harrow, one of Britain's most prestigious boarding schools. He was not known for discipline but was recognized for his strong memory and linguistic talent.

Royal Military Academy, Sandhurst

After several attempts, he passed the entrance exam for Sandhurst and trained as a cavalry officer. In 1895, Churchill graduated 20th out of 130 cadets and was commissioned as a second lieutenant in the 4th Queen’s Own Hussars.

Early Political Career

Churchill entered Parliament in 1900 as a Conservative MP but soon switched to the Liberal Party over disagreements about tariffs and free trade.

Positions Held:

  • Under-Secretary of State for the Colonies (1906–1908)
  • President of the Board of Trade (1908–1910)
  • Home Secretary (1910–1911)
  • First Lord of the Admiralty (1911–1915)

    • Reforms

    Churchill was heavily involved in:
  • Labor reforms
  • Social welfare programs
  • Improvements in working conditions
  • Modernization of the Royal Navy, including transitioning from coal to oil-powered ships.
World War I His biggest setback came with the Gallipoli Campaign (1915), a failed naval operation. Public criticism forced him to resign from the Admiralty.
He briefly served on the Western Front as a battalion commander before returning to government.

The Wilderness Years (1929-1939)

From 1929 onward, Churchill’s political influence declined. He took strong and unpopular positions, including:

  • Opposing Indian independence
  • Warning against the rise of Nazi Germany
  • Criticizing appeasement policies
Many in Parliament considered him outdated, but his warnings about Hitler would soon prove prophetic. During these years, he devoted himself to writing, producing historical works that earned great respect.

World War 2 and Leadership

When Neville Chamberlain resigned in 1940 after the collapse of the Norway campaign, Churchill became Prime Minister and Minister of Defence.
The Greatest Briton
His speeches, radio broadcasts, and defiant spirit galvanized Britain during its darkest hours:

  • Battle of Britain
  • Blitz bombings
  • Threat of invasion
  • >Struggle against Nazi Germany
    • Churchill maintained alliances with:
  • Franklin D. Roosevelt (United States)
  • Joseph Stalin (Soviet Union)
His leadership helped shape the strategy that led to Allied victory in Europe in 1945. However, despite winning the war, he lost the 1945 general election, as Britons chose a Labour government focused on domestic reform.

Death

Winston Churchill died on 24 January 1965 at age 90 after suffering a stroke. He was given a state funeral, one of the largest in history, attended by leaders from around the world.
He was buried at St Martin’s Church, Bladon, near his birthplace.
